# My reflection on the real problem about AI in the software industry

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!TsV3!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F42216983-42a9-4bb5-b94b-30f15a2f69f9_702x394.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!TsV3!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F42216983-42a9-4bb5-b94b-30f15a2f69f9_702x394.jpeg)

I’ve been trying for a while to correctly and logically organize my thoughts about the sector and this new “AI era.”

I’ve been working in software since 2013. I’ve worked in all kinds of software companies, including startups, scale-ups, corporations, small businesses, and consultancies (both the high-quality ones and the “body shops”).

During all this time, I’ve seen many of these companies generate code, most of which was “improvable.” Much of this sub-par code came from the need to rush just to get something out the door. Now, looking back and analyzing this, I can understand why many people feel that software development is slow. It is slow if we only consider the scenario I just mentioned; that is, if we only care about getting something out for yesterday when we’re already late.

Analyzing many of these cases in retrospect, here is what I’ve seen:

- **Sales teams** selling a non-existent product to a client without considering that this was a custom build for one specific customer. This meant the ROI for the company would be tiny or negligible, and they completely ignored the fact that the salesperson would still take their commission.
- **The product team** not understanding what “building a product” actually means. They think their job is to set self-inflicted deadlines for thousands of reasons that I don’t think are necessary to get into.
- **The C-level executive** who just had a magnificent idea and wants it for yesterday because today is already too late.
- **The C-level executive** who sets self-inflicted deadlines just to have a Gantt chart, claim things are going well, and show it off to “investors” or whoever else.
- **The Head of Product** confusing deliveries and dates with a product roadmap or a product strategy.
- **Someone in the company** who determined the team structure and created a distributed monolith, leaving everyone stuck in dependency relationships.
- ... (**an endless list**)

Most of the time when the code went wrong, it was because some of these factors were in the equation. It’s not the case every single time, but it is for the majority.

The sad part is that thinking that **increasing the speed of generating “code” will solve the problem of building software simply shows a lack of understanding of how software works**.

After much thought, I’ve realized that the problem was never the software. Instead, **the people working in software companies or managing software products DO NOT understand how software works**. They are using Maslow’s hammer to try to run a software company as if it were a factory.

It’s sad that, as in everything in life, **it’s easier to see the speck in someone else’s eye than the log in your own**. It’s sad that we blame a tool, or even a whole sector or industry, simply because we don’t even understand it.

That’s what my friend **[Fernando del Caz Babón](https://www.linkedin.com/in/fdelcaz/)** always says.

> If you work selling tiles, you’ll need to know what tiles are, how they’re used, who your potential customers are, what they like, and how they use tiles.

It’s no use selling software if you don’t know how your users actually use the software, what kind of software you’d like to create to solve these problems, and whether or not these problems have been solved.

Instead, many people in product, business, and sales don’t know the difference between output and outcome, and why a company has to go for outcomes and not outputs.

This is my reflection for the day. I know it doesn’t add much and it isn’t very grandiloquent. It’s not trying to sell you an opinion. It only reflects a bitter and sad truth: **most companies in the software industry are managed or led by people who don’t even know what they are managing**.

And remember, AI is not going to solve any of these problems. **Don’t forget that AI has proven to amplify things**, both the good and the bad. So, if this has been my experience, and I’m guessing a lot of people can relate to this, it’s safe to assume that many companies are about to become much more hostile places to work.
